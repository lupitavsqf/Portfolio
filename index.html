Crystallization and Dissolution, Accelerated

This series explores the interplay of light, color, and matter at the microscopic scale. Using polarized light microscopy, I recorded sodium chloride (NaCl, or table salt), magnesium sulfate heptahydrate (MgSO₄·7H₂O, or Epsom salts), sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO₃ or baking soda), and monoammonium phosphate (NH₄H₂PO₄) over 10 + hours of crystallization and dissolution. By accelerating the footage, the hidden behaviors of these salts—their growth, melting, and reformation—are revealed, along with the vibrant colors and patterns that emerge as polarized light passes through their crystalline structures. What unfolds is a dynamic choreography of light and matter, where scientific observation meets visual poetry.

  • Undergraduate thesis: A pragmatist approach for new dynamic ontologies in chemistry (2022)

    A pragmatist standpoint can be adopted legitimately in chemistry, whose knowledge is validated by the scientific method; observation perfectly matches the pragmatist concept of “sensible effects”. I go beyond the paradigm that conceives chemistry as totally reducible to physics to approach problems of 21st century philosophy of chemistry. Regarding time, I found out that time intervals in which emergent chemical phenomena occur must necessarily be continuums and the length of these intervals must be longer than a certain minimum determined by a perceptive apparatus. As a consequence, the properties that define chemical reactivity and identity are time-dependent. Since the facts we have access to are exhibited locally in our time dimension and those facts are the building blocks of the ontological frameworks in which reality emerges for us, I conclude that the chemical reality described in science must also be temporarily local.
